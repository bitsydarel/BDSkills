# Industry Product Leadership Models

How world-class product organizations make decisions. Use these frameworks to evaluate whether a proposal reflects proven leadership practices.

---

## Google

**Toothbrush Test** — "Is this something people will use once or twice a day?" Larry Page's filter for acquisitions and product investment. If a feature fails the toothbrush test, question its priority.

**OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)** — Stretch goals at 60-70% attainment = healthy ambition. 100% attainment signals sandbagging. Key results must be measurable, not activities.

**Data-Driven Product Culture** — Decisions backed by experiments. When data and intuition conflict, run another experiment. But recognize "not everything that counts can be counted."

**Kill Fast, Harvest Learning** — Google has killed 280+ products. Shutting down is not failure; failing to learn from shutdown is. Every killed product should produce a postmortem artifact.

**10x Rule** — Don't enter a market to be 10% better. Enter to be 10x better. If a proposal can't articulate a 10x improvement, it's incremental and vulnerable.

**Review question:** Does this proposal show evidence of stretch thinking, measurable outcomes, and willingness to kill what doesn't work?

## Netflix

**DHM Framework (Delight / Hard-to-copy / Margin-enhancing)** — Every product investment must satisfy all three:
- **Delight**: Does it create genuine user joy? (Not just utility)
- **Hard-to-copy**: Can competitors replicate this within 12 months? (Network effects, unique data, proprietary tech)
- **Margin-enhancing**: Does it improve unit economics or create leverage?

**GEM Metrics (Growth / Engagement / Monetization)** — The three lenses for product health. Growth without engagement is leaky. Engagement without monetization is a charity.

**Informed Captain** — One person owns the decision, informed by diverse perspectives. Not consensus, not autocracy. The captain makes the call and owns the outcome.

**Context Not Control** — Leaders set context (strategy, metrics, constraints); teams choose tactics. If you're telling teams *how* to solve problems, you're the bottleneck.

**A/B Testing Culture** — "Polish won't turn a negative signal positive." If the core value proposition doesn't resonate, no amount of UI refinement will save it. Test the concept before polishing the execution.

**Keeper Test** — "Would I fight to keep this person/project?" If not, it's time for a generous severance/sunset.

**Review question:** Does this proposal delight users in ways competitors can't easily copy while improving margins?

## Spotify

**DIBB Framework (Data → Insights → Beliefs → Bets)** — Forces evidence chains:
1. **Data**: What do we observe? (metrics, research, market signals)
2. **Insights**: What does the data tell us? (patterns, anomalies, opportunities)
3. **Beliefs**: What do we believe based on insights? (hypotheses, assumptions)
4. **Bets**: What are we willing to invest to test our beliefs?

**North Star Metric** — Time Spent Listening. Every team's work must connect to this. If a feature can't show a path to the North Star, it doesn't ship.

**Aligned Autonomy** — Company sets direction, squads choose the path. Alignment through shared North Star and strategy; autonomy in execution.

**Bet Board** — Track bets explicitly: hypothesis, investment, expected outcome, actual outcome. Makes learning systematic rather than accidental.

**Review question:** Can this proposal trace a clear Data → Insight → Belief → Bet chain, connected to the North Star Metric?

## Apple

**Radical Focus / Art of Saying No** — Apple's product line fits on one table. Saying no to 1,000 things for every yes. If a proposal doesn't serve the core experience, it's a distraction regardless of its standalone merit.

**DRI (Directly Responsible Individual)** — Every initiative has one name next to it. Not a committee, not "the team." One person accountable for outcome.

**Monday Executive Review** — Weekly cross-functional review with senior leadership. Products are scrutinized against boundary conditions. Surprises are organizational failures.

**Boundary Conditions** — Non-negotiable constraints defined upfront (performance, privacy, accessibility, battery impact). Features that violate boundary conditions don't ship, period.

**Functional Org, Single P&L** — No product-line silos. Design, engineering, marketing work across all products. Prevents local optimization at the expense of the whole.

**EVT/DVT/PVT Validation** — Engineering Validation Test → Design Validation Test → Production Validation Test. Staged validation with explicit gate criteria. Each stage has clear pass/fail.

**Experts-Lead-Experts** — Designers manage designers, engineers manage engineers. Domain expertise in the management chain, not generalist managers making technical calls.

**Review question:** Does this proposal meet boundary conditions, have a clear DRI, and pass staged validation gates?

## Microsoft (Satya Nadella Era)

**Growth Mindset** — Shift from "know-it-all" to "learn-it-all." Proposals should demonstrate learning orientation — what experiments are planned, what assumptions need testing.

**Power Metrics** — Adoption, usage, and satisfaction over revenue. Revenue is a lagging indicator of product health. Lead with user value; revenue follows.

**Customer Zero** — Use your own products first and ruthlessly. Internal dogfooding as the first quality gate. If your own team won't use it, customers won't either.

**Create Clarity / Generate Energy / Deliver Success** — Leadership framework:
- **Create Clarity**: Can you articulate the strategy in one sentence?
- **Generate Energy**: Does the team believe this matters?
- **Deliver Success**: Are there concrete milestones with accountability?

**Review question:** Does this proposal show a learning orientation, lead with usage metrics, and demonstrate internal conviction?

## Modern Product Frameworks

**Marty Cagan's Four Risks** — Value, Usability, Feasibility, Viability. The foundation of this skill's evaluation model. Every proposal must address all four.

**Teresa Torres' Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)** — Desired Outcome → Opportunities → Solutions → Experiments. Forces teams to explore multiple solutions before committing. Anti-pattern: jumping from outcome to single solution.

**RICE Scoring** — Reach × Impact × Confidence / Effort. Useful for prioritization but dangerous as sole decision framework (ignores strategic alignment and risk).

**North Star Metric Framework** — One metric that captures the core value your product delivers. All team metrics should be leading indicators of or contributors to the North Star.

**Product-Led Growth (PLG) Metrics** — Activation rate, time-to-value, expansion revenue, viral coefficient. PLG products need instrumentation to track these from day one.

**Product Sense Frameworks** — CIRCLES (Comprehend, Identify, Report, Cut, List, Evaluate, Summarize) and GAME (Goals, Actions, Metrics, Evaluations) for structured product thinking.

## Applying Industry Models in Reviews

When reviewing, check whether the proposal's approach is consistent with at least one proven industry model:

1. **Is there a clear evidence chain?** (Spotify DIBB)
2. **Is the value hard to copy?** (Netflix DHM)
3. **Is there a 10x improvement claim?** (Google 10x, or justified incremental)
4. **Are boundary conditions defined?** (Apple)
5. **Is there a learning plan?** (Microsoft Growth Mindset)

A proposal that can't connect to any established framework may be operating on assumption alone.
