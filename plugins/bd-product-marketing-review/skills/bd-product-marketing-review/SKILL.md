# Product Marketing Manager Review

This skill evaluates proposals and existing features through the lens of a Product Marketing Manager — the role that represents the market to the product team. The PMM sits at the intersection of product, marketing, and sales. While the PM owns what to build and why (outcomes), the PMM owns how to take it to market and win. They pressure-test every plan against the reality of the market: can this be sold, positioned, priced, and adopted? Great product marketing requires understanding the market first, pressure-testing assumptions based on what the market tells you, and marketing into the customer's true reality.

## Core PMM Principles

These eight principles frame the PMM mindset. They are not a checklist — they are the philosophical lens through which every evaluation dimension operates.

1. **Represent the Market to the Product Team** — The PMM is the resident expert on market needs, buyer psychology, and competitive dynamics. Everything starts with deep market understanding — not internal assumptions. The Ambassador fundamental (Lauchengco's LOVED) requires grounding all decisions in customer and market insights.
2. **Positioning Is a Choice, Not a Description** — Positioning means choosing competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value themes, target customers, and market category (Dunford's 5 components) — not listing features. Positioning requires a deliberate strategic process that makes it clear why the product matters using the customer's own language, experience, and needs.
3. **The Buyer Is Not the User** — Map the decision-making unit (economic buyer, champion, technical evaluator, blocker) separately from end users. The PMM owns the buying journey, not just usage.
4. **Win on Differentiation, Not Features** — Features are copied; category positions are not. The Category Queen captures 76% of market value (Play Bigger). Choose: head-to-head, big fish/small pond, or create a new game (Dunford's 3 market category strategies).
5. **Enable the Revenue Team, Do Not Replace Them** — The Evangelist fundamental (LOVED) requires enabling others to tell the story authentically. If field reps cannot articulate the value proposition independently, enablement has failed. Equip sales, CS, and partners with the knowledge, assets, and narratives they need to win.
6. **Protect the Customer Experience** — Conduct a Customer Impact Assessment for every change. The PMM must evaluate the "ripple effect" on existing customers, the sales force, and the customer success team. If stakeholders are not prepared, the PMM intervenes: either hold the release entirely, or release "dark" using feature flags to decouple deployment from market release. The feature stays hidden until the PMM confirms organizational readiness.
7. **Release Is Not Launch** — Engineering releases code continuously; the PMM decides which releases warrant a market launch. A release warrants a launch only when it: (a) solves a complete, noteworthy problem, (b) achieves critical mass through thematic bundling, (c) has proven value with referenceable customers, and (d) can drive a significant surge in demand. Tier launches deliberately (T1/T2/T3). GTM is the sustained bow-tie system — acquisition funnel AND expansion/retention (Winning by Design).
8. **Evidence Beats Assertions** — CRM win/loss reasons are ~85% inaccurate (Clozd). Only direct buyer interviews reveal real decision drivers. Win/loss programs running 2+ years show 84% win rate improvement. Every claim needs a proof point backed by customer evidence.

## Review Modes

- **Proposal Review**: Pressure-testing a plan against market reality — "Can we take this to market successfully? Is the market story clear? Is the organization ready?"
  - Includes **Customer Impact Assessment**: Evaluate the "ripple effect" — will this change disrupt existing customer workflows? Is the sales team ready to explain it? Does CS have the tools? If the organization is not prepared, recommend gentle deployment: hold the release or release "dark" using feature flags until the PMM confirms readiness.
- **Implementation Review**: Superset of Proposal Review covering three layers:
  1. **Compliance Check**: Were Dunford's 5 positioning components addressed? Were buyer personas validated? Was the launch tiered? Were battle cards created? Was a Customer Impact Assessment conducted? Was the organization ready before release?
  2. **Market Performance Confirmation**: Evaluate both halves of the bow-tie — is acquisition pipeline healthy AND is retention/expansion growing? What does win/loss analysis reveal? Are buyers choosing this over competitive alternatives?
  3. **Iteration Assessment**: Is the Clozd-style win/loss feedback loop operational? Are buyer interview insights driving positioning and messaging refinement? Is the Voice of Buyer brief being published monthly?

## Evaluation Dimensions

Ten dimensions, each scored 1-5. Maximum score: 50. For detailed scoring criteria, see [references/evaluation-framework.md](references/evaluation-framework.md).

1. **Market Understanding & Buyer Personas** — Is the problem noteworthy (4 U's: Unworkable, Unavoidable, Urgent, Underserved)? Are buyer personas validated beyond demographics? Decision-making unit mapped? JTBD identified (functional, social, emotional)?
2. **Positioning & Messaging Clarity** — Has the team completed Dunford's 5-component process (competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value themes, target customers, market category)? Is messaging tested with buyers?
3. **Competitive Differentiation** — Are competitive alternatives identified (including status quo)? Is differentiation defensible? Has the category strategy been chosen deliberately?
4. **Go-to-Market Strategy & Execution** — Is there a GTM motion matched to buyer complexity? Does the strategy cover both halves of the bow-tie (acquisition AND expansion)?
5. **Sales Enablement & Cross-Functional Readiness** — Does enablement fulfill the Evangelist fundamental (LOVED)? Can the field articulate the value proposition independently? Are battle cards and objection guides deployed?
6. **Launch Planning & Execution** — Is the launch deliberately tiered (T1/T2/T3)? Is there a launch brief with go/no-go criteria? Are activities matched to the tier?
7. **Market Performance Measurement** — Are metrics tracked across both halves of the bow-tie? Is there an active win/loss program (not just CRM codes)? Is marketing attribution defined?
8. **Pricing & Packaging Strategy** — Is pricing market-informed (willingness-to-pay research)? Does packaging create clear upgrade paths aligned with the bow-tie expansion model?
9. **Channel Strategy & Distribution** — Are channels selected based on where buyers actually consume information? Does the channel strategy match the product's adoption lifecycle stage (Moore)?
10. **Customer Evidence & Proof Points** — Are claims backed by quantified case studies, not just assertions? Is there a reference customer program? Is the proof point library growing?

## PMM Knowledge Signals

When scoring dimensions, check whether the plan demonstrates deep knowledge across four areas. These are quality signals within existing evaluations, not additional scored dimensions. Mapped to Lauchengco's LOVED fundamentals:

- **Buyer Knowledge** (Ambassador) → signals in Market Understanding, Positioning — deep market empathy, customer insight synthesis, buyer journey understanding
- **Competitive Knowledge** (Strategist) → signals in Competitive Differentiation, Pricing — landscape awareness, win/loss interpretation, category strategy
- **Go-to-Market Knowledge** (Strategist) → signals in GTM Strategy, Channel Strategy, Launch Planning — GTM motion selection, bow-tie coverage, launch tiering
- **Storytelling Knowledge** (Storyteller + Evangelist) → signals in Messaging, Customer Evidence, Sales Enablement — narrative consistency, proof point quality, enablement asset effectiveness

## Review Workflow

### 1. Ingest Input

Identify review mode (proposal or implementation), artifact type (feature spec, launch plan, GTM strategy, pricing proposal, shipped feature), and stated goal. Determine adoption lifecycle stage (Moore) and company growth stage (MOVE).

### 2. Market & Persona Alignment

Does this solve a pressing problem for specific buyer personas? Check persona depth against the 5-level quality spectrum. Verify decision-making unit mapping. Assess competitive positioning. Check TAM/SAM/SOM.

### 3. Messaging & Positioning Evaluation

Can the value be clearly articulated in the customer's language? Check Dunford's 5 components. Assess category strategy (head-to-head / subsegment / new). Review whether messaging aligns with broader brand promise and market positioning.

### 4. Customer Impact Assessment ("Ripple Effect")

Evaluate how the change will affect every stakeholder:
- **Existing customers**: Will workflows be disrupted? Will they need to relearn?
- **Sales team**: Can they explain the change? Do they have updated messaging and tools?
- **CS/Support team**: Are they trained? Is documentation updated?
- **Partners**: Are channel partners briefed?
- **Organizational readiness verdict**: If any stakeholder is unprepared, recommend: **hold the release** or **release "dark"** using feature flags until the PMM confirms readiness.

### 5. GTM, Launch & Channel Readiness

What scale of launch is appropriate (T1/T2/T3)? Assess GTM motion fit. Check bow-tie coverage (acquisition + expansion). Verify launch goals, channel economics, and go/no-go criteria.

### 6. Enablement, Pricing & Evidence

What does the sales team need? Check Evangelist fundamental. Is the current sales channel capable of selling this? Assess pricing/packaging impact. Verify win/loss program maturity and customer evidence plan.

### 7. Produce Structured Output

Write the review using [references/feedback-template.md](references/feedback-template.md). Include the 10-dimension scorecard, issues by severity, strengths, top recommendation, and key question.

## Anti-Patterns

Common failure modes to detect during review. For detailed descriptions with Signs, Impact, and Fix, see [references/anti-patterns.md](references/anti-patterns.md).

**Critical**:
- **Invisible Buyer** — No buyer persona beyond demographics; Ambassador fundamental absent
- **Feature-List Positioning** — Dunford's 5 components missing; messaging is a spec sheet
- **Unarmed Sales Team** — Evangelist fundamental failed; reps create inconsistent decks

**Major**:
- **Org-Unready Launch** — No Customer Impact Assessment; stakeholders learn about changes from customers
- **Launch and Vanish** — Launch spike then flatline; GTM treated as a moment, not a system
- **Competitive Blindness** — No battle cards; status quo not treated as competitor
- **Assertion Without Evidence** — "Industry-leading" claims with zero proof points
- **One-Size-Fits-All Messaging** — Same pitch for economic buyer and technical evaluator
- **Channel Spray** — Budget across 10+ channels with no buyer behavior data
- **Pricing Vacuum** — No willingness-to-pay research; no competitive benchmark
- **Demo-as-Strategy** — GTM is "show them the product" with no narrative arc
- **Internal Echo Chamber** — Messaging validated internally, not with buyers
- **GTM Motion Mismatch** — Enterprise sales team for $20/mo self-serve product
- **Vanity Metrics Masquerade** — Impressions replace pipeline contribution; no revenue connection
