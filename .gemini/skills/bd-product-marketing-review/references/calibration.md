# Calibration Guide

What excellent PMM rigor looks like — framework-based quality spectrums and reference tools for each evaluation dimension.

## Positioning Quality Spectrum (Dunford's Framework)

| Level | Quality | What It Looks Like |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Feature List | "We have AI analytics, real-time dashboards, 50+ integrations" |
| 2 | Generic Benefit | "We help companies make better decisions with data" |
| 3 | Partial Framework | Category and target defined, but competitive alternatives are phantom competitors or value themes untested |
| 4 | Complete Framework | All 5 Dunford components completed, market category deliberately chosen (head-to-head, subsegment, or new) |
| 5 | Validated Framework | Framework complete + messaging tested with 5+ buyers + sales confirms "prospects immediately get it" + win/loss confirms positioning resonance |

## Dunford's 5-Component Quick Reference

| Component | Question | Strong Signal | Weak Signal |
|-----------|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Competitive Alternatives | What would buyers do without us? | Real alternatives from buyer interviews (including status quo) | Phantom competitors they never actually lose to |
| Unique Attributes | What can we do that alternatives can't? | Third-party validated, evidence-backed | Undifferentiated or unsubstantiated claims |
| Value Themes | Why do those attributes matter? | 2-4 themes mapped to buyer outcomes | Features described without customer benefit |
| Target Customers | Who cares most about this value? | Specific characteristics from best-fit customer analysis | "Everyone" or demographic-only segmentation |
| Market Category | What context makes our value obvious? | Category puts strengths at center, strategy deliberate | Default category from product history |

## Dunford's 3 Market Category Strategies

| Strategy | When to Choose | Risk Level | Example |
|----------|---------------|------------|---------|
| Head-to-Head | Clear advantages over existing leaders | High — fighting established players | Competing directly with Salesforce CRM |
| Big Fish, Small Pond | Subsegment has different needs | Moderate — own a niche first | "Customer messaging for bootstrapped SaaS" (Userlist) |
| Create a New Game | Genuinely novel, resources for market education | Highest — must prove category deserves to exist | Slack creating "team collaboration" category |

## Problem Noteworthiness Assessment

The PMM must assess whether a problem is truly worth solving AND worth launching before committing market resources.

### The 4 U's Framework

| Dimension | Question | Strong Signal | Weak Signal |
|-----------|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Unworkable** | Does the current situation cause a broken process with measurable consequences? | "Teams lose 8 hours/week to manual reconciliation" | "It would be nice to improve this" |
| **Unavoidable** | Do all participants in the target segment encounter this? | "Every mid-market finance team hits this during month-end close" | "Some users occasionally mention it" |
| **Urgent** | Is it a top-3 priority with active budget allocation? | "3 of 5 prospects named this in discovery calls unprompted" | "They agreed it's a problem when we asked" |
| **Underserved** | Are current solutions inadequate, evidenced by workarounds? | "Teams built spreadsheet macros and hired contractors to work around it" | "Existing tools handle it adequately" |

Problem scoring high on all 4 U's = strong market signal. Fewer than 2 U's = risky investment.

### Blatant/Critical vs Latent/Aspirational

| Problem Type | Customer Behavior | PMM Implication |
|-------------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Blatant + Critical** | Actively searching for solutions to a mission-critical issue | Easiest to market — demand exists. Focus on positioning and differentiation. |
| **Blatant + Aspirational** | Actively looking but not mission-critical (nice-to-have) | Moderate demand. Must prove ROI to justify purchase. |
| **Latent + Critical** | Unaware of the problem but it's causing real damage | Requires market education. Category creation opportunity (Play Bigger). High investment. |
| **Latent + Aspirational** | Unaware and not mission-critical | Hardest to market. Avoid unless creating a new category with significant resources. |

### Evidence of Workarounds / "Solution Hacks"

The strongest validation that a problem is real is observing what customers do TODAY to work around it. Workarounds include: spreadsheet macros, manual processes, hired contractors, cobbled-together tool chains, internal scripts, or "we just live with it." If customers have invested time and money in workarounds, the problem is validated by behavior, not just words.

### Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) — 3 Dimensions

| Dimension | Question | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Functional** | What task does the buyer need to accomplish? | "Reconcile invoices with purchase orders by month-end" |
| **Social** | How does the buyer want to be perceived? | "Be seen as the leader who modernized the finance team" |
| **Emotional** | How does the buyer want to feel? | "Feel confident that month-end close won't surface errors in board meetings" |

**PMM insight**: Functional jobs are table stakes. Social and emotional jobs drive differentiation and premium pricing. Messaging that addresses all three dimensions converts at higher rates.

### 4 W's of Problem Framing

> **Who** [specific buyer persona] struggles with **What** [specific problem] **Where** [context/trigger] because **Why** [root cause], causing [measurable impact].

**Weak**: "Companies need better analytics." (No who, no what, no where, no why.)

**Strong**: "Mid-market finance directors struggle with invoice reconciliation during month-end close because their ERP lacks automated matching, causing 8 hours/week of manual work and a 12% error rate that delays board reporting."

### Launch Noteworthiness Test

A release warrants a market launch only when the underlying problem meets the noteworthiness bar — (a) problem scores high on the 4 U's, (b) feature solves a complete problem (not a partial fix), (c) referenceable customers validate the solution, (d) the launch can drive a measurable demand surge. If the problem is Latent or scores low on the 4 U's, the launch strategy must include significant market education.

## Buyer Persona Quality Spectrum

| Level | Quality | What's Included |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| 1 | Label | "Marketing Manager" |
| 2 | Demographic | Job title + company size + industry |
| 3 | Behavioral | + Goals, frustrations, daily activities, information sources |
| 4 | Buying | + Buying triggers, objections, evaluation process, decision-making unit role, typical timeline |
| 5 | Validated | + Win/loss data, competitive preferences, actual close rates by persona |

## Bow-Tie Model Quick Reference

```
ACQUISITION (Left)          EXPANSION (Right)
Awareness ──┐          ┌── Onboarding
Education ──┤          ├── Adoption
Selection ──┤  ◆ COMMIT ├── Recurring Impact
            └──────────┘├── Expansion
                        └── Advocacy
```

| Side | Key PMM Activities | Key Metrics |
|------|-------------------|-------------|
| Left (Acquisition) | Positioning, content, demand gen, competitive | Pipeline, win rate, CAC, sales cycle |
| Center (Commit) | Sales enablement, pricing, packaging | ACV, close rate, CAC payback |
| Right (Expansion) | Customer evidence, adoption content, upsell messaging | NRR, expansion revenue, logo retention, advocacy |

**Critical insight**: Mature companies get 2/3 of growth from expansion (right side). Early-stage companies get 88% from acquisition (left side). The PMM review must evaluate BOTH sides.

## Win/Loss Maturity Spectrum (Clozd Framework)

| Level | Approach | Data Quality | Insight Value |
|-------|----------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | None | No data | Zero — guessing |
| 2 | CRM Codes | Sales rep self-report | Very low — 85% inaccurate per Clozd |
| 3 | Sales Debrief | Internal team discussion | Low — filtered through sales lens |
| 4 | Buyer Surveys | Automated questionnaires | Moderate — limited depth |
| 5 | Buyer Interviews (Flex) | AI-driven interviews | Good — scalable, better than surveys |
| 6 | Buyer Interviews (Live) | Neutral third-party, 30-45 min | Excellent — gold standard for complex B2B |
| 7 | Systematic Program | Live interviews + theme tagging + monthly Voice of Buyer brief + quarterly reports | Highest — 84% win rate improvement for 2+ year programs |

## Launch Tier Decision Matrix (Forrester/Intercom Model)

| Criterion | T1 (Strategic) | T2 (Major) | T3 (Enhancement) |
|-----------|---------------|------------|-------------------|
| Functionality | Net new, strategically important | Net new or significant update | Minor update, table stakes |
| Market Impact | Top-of-funnel, new customer acquisition | Existing customer expansion | Existing customer retention |
| Who Should Know | Everyone internally + externally | Relevant teams + affected customers | Affected customers |
| Activities | Press, video, events, exec sponsorship, full enablement, analyst briefing | Blog, web page, sales collateral, email, training | Changelog, in-app notification, docs |
| Launch Brief | Full brief with all 10 sections + DACI | Abbreviated brief, 4-6 key sections | Minimal — changelog entry |
| Go/No-Go | Formal review 4-6 weeks before | Informal review 2 weeks before | No formal gate |
| Post-Launch | Full retrospective at 30 days | Light review at 30 days | Adoption metrics only |

## LOVED Fundamentals Assessment

| Fundamental | Strong Signal | Weak Signal |
|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| **Ambassador** | Continuous market listening, buyer interviews, VoC program, customer advisory board | Assumptions-driven, no customer contact, internal-only research |
| **Strategist** | 12-18 month positioning vision, GTM orchestrated, stage-appropriate MOVE strategy | Reactive to requests, no long-term vision, treats all launches equally |
| **Storyteller** | Consistent narrative across all touchpoints, messaging tested with buyers, category narrative defined | Inconsistent messaging, feature-list descriptions, no buyer validation |
| **Evangelist** | Sales can articulate independently, customer advocates recruited, authentic word-of-mouth | Sales creates own materials, no advocacy program, forced/inauthentic promotion |

## Customer Impact Assessment Framework

The PMM conducts this assessment for every product change to evaluate organizational readiness.

| Stakeholder | Assessment Questions | Ready? |
|-------------|---------------------|--------|
| **Existing Customers** | Will workflows change? Is relearning required? Will it feel like a "moving target"? | |
| **Sales Team** | Can reps explain the change? Are updated materials available? | |
| **CS/Support** | Is documentation updated? Are support macros ready? Can they handle questions without escalation? | |
| **Partners/Channel** | Are partner enablement materials updated? Are channel teams briefed? | |
| **Internal Org** | Has the change been communicated at all-hands/team meetings? | |

### Deployment Decision Matrix

| Org Readiness | Customer Impact | Recommended Action |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Ready | Low (minor UI change) | Release normally |
| Ready | High (workflow change) | Release with coordinated communication |
| Not Ready | Low | Release "dark" with feature flags; enable when ready |
| Not Ready | High | Hold the release entirely until ready |

### "Dark Release" via Feature Flags

- Feature flags (toggles/gates) decouple deployment from release — code goes to production but stays hidden
- PMM controls the "on" switch, not engineering — the feature goes live when the market org is ready
- Enables A/B testing with a subset of users, shipping incomplete work safely, and instant rollback
- Mobile caveat: Some app stores have strict policies about hidden/dark features — ensure compliance

## Complete PMM Toolkit

All techniques a PMM reviewer checks for, organized by application scope.

### Tactical (Campaign/Asset Level)

- Messaging hierarchy (tagline, value props, proof points)
- Battle cards per competitor (Klue/Crayon format)
- Sales pitch deck and demo script (Dunford "Sales Pitch" methodology)
- Objection handling guide
- ROI calculator or business case template
- Case study with quantified results
- Product one-pager per persona

### Operational (Launch/Quarter Level)

- Positioning framework document (Dunford 5-component canvas)
- Launch brief with tier classification (T1/T2/T3)
- Demand generation campaign plan (mapped to bow-tie left side)
- Content calendar mapped to buyer journey stages
- Win/loss analysis reports (Clozd methodology)
- Channel performance dashboard
- Sales enablement readiness checklist
- Analyst briefing deck
- Pricing and packaging rationale

### Strategic (Annual/Market Level)

- TAM/SAM/SOM analysis
- Validated buyer persona documentation (Level 5: win/loss validated)
- Competitive intelligence program (Clozd 4 pillars)
- GTM motion definition and rationale (MOVE stage-appropriate)
- Category strategy decision (Dunford: head-to-head / subsegment / new category)
- Bow-tie revenue model with both-side metrics
- Customer advisory board program
- Analyst relations strategy
- Partner ecosystem strategy
- Market performance scorecard (acquisition + expansion KPIs)
